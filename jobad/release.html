Normal generation of locally compact groups

A. Eisenmann and N. Monod
EPFL, Switzerland
Abstract.

It is a well-known open problem since the 1970s whether a finitely generated perfect group can be normally generated by a single element or not. We prove that the topological version of this problem has an affirmative answer as long as we exclude infinite discrete quotients (which is probably a necessary restriction).

1. Introduction

Let GGG be a perfect group. Can GGG be normally generated by a single element?

Equivalently: can GGG be made trivial by adding a single relation? In other words yet: does GGG contain some element not belonging to any proper normal subgroup?


This is well-known to be true when GGG is finite (see e.g. [, 4.2] for a stronger fact). For infinite groups, it is a long-standing open problem attributed to J. Wiegold; see [, FP14] and [, 5.52]. The group GGG is assumed to be finitely generated in order to avoid obvious counter-examples (infinite direct sums of perfect groups). It is widely believed to be false: for instance, Lennox and Wiegold conjecture that a free product of nnn finite groups cannot be normally generated by less than n/2n2n/2 elements []. A completely different potential source of counter-examples is proposed in [].


The question can be asked more generally for topological groups GGG (which we always suppose Hausdorff). Here GGG is called perfect if it has no non-trivial continuous homomorphism to an abelian topological group, or equivalently if its commutator subgroup is dense. Likewise, it is deemed normally generated by a single element g∈GgGg\in G if the smallest closed normal subgroup containing ggg is GGG itself.

If GGG has an infinite discrete quotient group, then the putative counter-examples for abstract groups will of course be a fortiori counter-examples for GGG itself. Rather surprisingly, the question has a positive answer when we exclude such quotients:

Theorem 1.

Let GGG be a compactly generated locally compact group without infinite discrete quotient. If GGG is perfect, then it is normally generated by a single element.

This result uses structure theory, both classical [] and very recent [].

Remark 2.

Compact generation is the replacement for the necessary assumption of finite generation. It remains necessary even when there is no infinite discrete quotient, as shown by an example constructed for us by Y. de Cornulier, see Section 3 below.

3. On compact generation

Yves de Cornulier has kindly allowed us to present his example of a perfect locally compact group GGG which is not normally generated by a single element although it has no infinite discrete (group) quotient.

Assume that FFF is a finite perfect group with a subgroup K<FKFK<F such that

  1. ((i))

    FFF is normally generated by KKK,

  2. ((ii))

    FFF is not normally generated by any single element of KKK.

Let GGG be the group of sequences in FFF that are ultimately in KKK. The “restricted product” locally compact group topology on GGG is defined by the identity neighbourhood base Un<K𝐍subscriptUnsuperscriptK𝐍U_{n}<K^{\mathbf{N}} consisting of sequences that are trivial on the first nnn coordinates. The perfect group F(𝐍)superscriptF𝐍F^{{(\mathbf{N})}} of finitely supported sequences is then dense in GGG, so that GGG is perfect. Moreover, condition ((ii)) ensures that GGG cannot be normally generated by a single element.

Consider any discrete quotient of GGG. Its kernel must contain some UnsubscriptUnU_{n}; being normal, it follows by condition ((i)) that this quotient is a quotient of FnsuperscriptFnF^{n}, thus it is finite.


It remains to see that a pair K<FKFK<F as above indeed exists. Let ccc be an element of order two in a non-abelian finite simple group SSS. Set F=S3FsuperscriptS3F=S^{3} and let KKK be the (Klein) subgroup generated by (1,c,c)1cc(1,c,c) and (c,c,1)cc1(c,c,1). Since the maximal normal subgroups of FFF are the kernels of the three canonical projections F→Snormal-→FSF\to S, which do not contain KKK, condition ((i)) holds. On the other hand, any element of KKK is in one of these kernels, whence condition ((ii)).
